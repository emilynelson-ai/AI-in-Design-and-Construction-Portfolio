Example 1

Togal.AI

Type: AI Estimating Tool
Focus: Takeoff + Cost Workflow

Workflow Reflection

Historically, human estimators have done the core takeoff and pricing work, which is then back-checked by a senior estimator or peer. AI-enabled estimating tools may shift that process so AI handles more of the heavy-lifting grunt work while the human estimator audits the output, questions assumptions, and makes professional judgment calls.

How the Role May Evolve

In the future, the strongest estimating professionals may be the ones who focus less on manual quantity production and more on managing risk, validating assumptions, and offering value-engineering or cost-saving suggestions. That future role feels closer to a preconstruction manager than a traditional estimator.

Industry Impact

AI tools could also help estimators keep pace with fast-moving design changes. In the past, by the time an estimate was complete, the design had already changed and the model was out of date. AI-assisted estimating may allow estimators to stay more integrated with the iterative design process and help the industry keep up with growing bid volume despite a shortage of experienced estimators.

Client and Market Effects

Clients will increasingly have AI tools at their fingertips as well, which means they may challenge estimates more rigorously using AI agents. That could push contractors toward greater transparency and more “open book” pricing approaches.

Ethical Considerations

  • Estimators still need to stand behind the proposals issued to clients.
  • Blaming bid errors on AI software is unethical.
  • Human judgment remains essential in how quantities, assumptions, and pricing are validated.

Security Considerations

Another important issue is design privacy and proprietary information. Many firms have details, drawings, and internal strategies that should not be freely exposed through cloud-based tools. That makes software security and data governance critical when adopting AI-enabled estimating platforms.

Example 2

Review of Mini-Agents

Type: GPT Evaluation
Focus: Personal + AEC Use

Crochet Companion

I intentionally tested a GPT in an area where I already had significant prior knowledge. Crochet Companion performed better than I expected. It asked clarifying questions without being prompted, provided detailed and accurate technique explanations, and showed how well a GPT can guide a user through a task-based hobby workflow.

Architects Toolbox – AIA Contract Guide

The AEC GPT was especially strong. It handled multiple AIA contracts, different delivery models, and common project issues well. It was able to point to where information could be found within AIA agreements and offered practical contractual guidance for issues like nonconforming work and change-order review.

My Reflection

Both GPTs exceeded my expectations, but in different ways. The personal-use GPT showed the value of thoughtful interaction design and clarifying questions. The AEC GPT showed how effective a mini-agent can be when it has strong domain knowledge. One improvement I would still want from the AEC GPT is a more explicit opposing-argument view, since construction issues are rarely black and white.

Example 3

Using a Book Review GPT

Type: Learning + Synthesis
Book: The Myth of the Nice Girl

Book Referenced

In this use case, I used a Book Review GPT interaction focused on The Myth of the Nice Girl. The GPT was used as a study and synthesis tool to condense key ideas from the book into a more digestible format.

How I Used It

Rather than reading the GPT output as a substitute for the book, I approached it as a way to surface major themes, reinforce key points, and accelerate reflection. This showed how GPTs can support comprehension and professional learning by organizing ideas, summarizing content, and helping users identify the most relevant takeaways.

Takeaway

This use case showed me that GPTs can be valuable learning companions when used responsibly. Their strength is not just in summarizing text, but in helping users interact with material more quickly and reflect on how those lessons apply to their own professional development.

Example 4

Create Mini-Agent / GPT

Custom GPT: AIA Conflict Navigator
Focus: Contract-Based Conflict Analysis

Goal

I created AIA Conflict Navigator to address a recurring problem in design and construction: when conflict arises, ego and emotion often overshadow resolution. The goal of the GPT is to bring the discussion back to the contract and provide the perspective of all project stakeholders.

Who It Serves

The mini-agent is intended for owners, designers, and contractors. It is built to understand AIA contracts, project-administration disputes, and the typical motivations of each party when conflicts emerge on a project.

Expected Output

The GPT is designed to provide a 360-degree analysis, identify likely contractual pathways for resolution, and rate the strength of each party’s position so the project team can move toward a practical off-ramp.

Methodology

I structured the GPT to respond through a repeatable nine-part method: conflict summary, contract issues triggered, owner perspective, architect perspective, contractor perspective, integrated analysis, contractual resolution path, recommended next moves, and a final risk snapshot.

Performance Reflection

The GPT performed well in producing detailed responses and consistently followed the intended framework. It handled changing delivery methods and complex consultant scenarios well, though I would still like shorter outputs and more direct citations to specific AIA sections for easier back-checking.

Ethical Guardrails

Because the GPT is publicly available, I built in guardrails such as not providing legal advice, not fabricating AIA clause language, and explicitly flagging when facts are incomplete. I also see harmful misuse and overreliance as important ethical risks for public-facing mini-agents.
