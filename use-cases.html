Custom AI Agent Use Case

AIA Conflict Navigator

AIA Conflict Navigator is a custom GPT designed to analyze project conflicts through standard AIA contract frameworks and provide a balanced, 360-degree perspective for the Owner, Architect, and Contractor. The goal is to help teams move past emotion-driven disputes and toward practical, contract-based resolution pathways.

Problem Addressed

When conflict arises on a project, ego and emotion can overshadow resolution. This mini-agent uses the contract to re-center the discussion on roles, obligations, and practical next steps.

Who It Serves

Owners, designers, and contractors can all use the tool to better understand where perspectives align, where they conflict, and how disputes might be resolved.

Expected Output

The tool produces a 360-degree analysis, resolution pathways, and a strength rating of each party’s position to help move the project forward.

Use Case Overview

Why This Agent Matters in AEC

Contract interpretation and project conflict are recurring challenges in design and construction. This custom GPT demonstrates how AI can support conflict analysis without replacing professional judgment or legal counsel.

Core Function

The agent is built to analyze standard AIA agreements and explain how the Owner, Architect, and Contractor would each likely approach conflict resolution under common AIA risk-allocation patterns. It is intended as an educational and project-administration support tool, not a source of legal advice.

Knowledge and Context

To be effective, the mini-agent needs familiarity with AIA contracts, project administration workflows, and the typical motivations of owners, designers, and contractors when responding to issues such as payment disputes, delays, change orders, defective work, and substantial completion disagreements.

Response Method

How the Agent Structures Analysis

The custom GPT was instructed to follow a consistent nine-part framework so every dispute is analyzed in a clear, balanced, and repeatable way.

Conflict Summary

Restates the dispute in neutral language and identifies missing facts that could materially change the analysis.

Contract Issues Triggered

Identifies notice, timing, payment, design responsibility, change authorization, delays, defective work, and other contract administration issues.

Stakeholder Perspectives

Explains the likely position of the Owner, Architect, and Contractor, including business concerns, contract arguments, risk exposure, and documentation needs.

Integrated Resolution Path

Shows where parties align, where they are vulnerable, and what sequence of notices, directives, claims, mediation, or escalation may apply.

Assignment Goals

Design Intent for the Mini-Agent

The project was developed as a contract-analysis mini-agent that could support project teams by translating complicated disputes into a more organized, actionable, and collaborative framework.

What the Agent Produces

  • A neutral summary of the conflict
  • A 360-degree perspective across all three major parties
  • Practical next steps for de-escalation and resolution
  • A risk snapshot rating each party's position as strong, moderate, or weak

Key Guardrails

  • Does not provide legal advice
  • Does not fabricate AIA clause language
  • Flags when facts are incomplete
  • Notes when contract editions, amendments, or supplementary conditions may change the outcome
Performance Reflection

What Worked and What Could Improve

In testing, the agent performed well in producing detailed outputs and following the required structure. It adapted to different project delivery scenarios and handled complex consultant-related issues effectively, but there is still room to improve clarity and speed.

1

Strengths

The tool consistently followed the nine-step framework and generated detailed, organized responses across multiple test scenarios.

2

Limitations

The responses were often long, and the agent would benefit from citing specific AIA sections more directly for faster back-checking.

3

Future Potential

With additional training, the tool could be refined to produce shorter table-based outputs and eventually anticipate future project conflicts using project progress data.
